ACOUSTICS

AHEAD OF THE CURVE

John Storyk elaborates on NIRO, a cutting-edge acoustics engine that promises to transform the way modern studios are designed.

JULY 27, 2022

A

s described in the main article, WSDG and John Storyk used groundbreaking software in the design of Rue Boyer Studios, called NIRO. Storyk elaborates on what it is, why it is essential, and how it was applied.

“Until 30 years ago we only had a 100-year old formula, the Sabine equation, to calculate reverb times, which was useful, but also of limited use in smaller rooms. Studios were designed by intuition, guesswork, measurement, using one’s ears, and then trying to improve the next studio.”

“When we were at last able to make accurate real time measurements that paralleled what we were hearing, it became an extraordinary breakthrough in small room acoustic design. Time delay spectrometry introduced this. Accurate measurements led to accurate predictive software, but for the most part this is limited to analysis ABOVE the Schroeder frequency.”

SCHROEDER FREQUENCY

Every room has a unique set of resonant frequencies, commonly refered to as room modes. As you rise in frequency, the room modes start to bunch together. Eventually the intervals between them is so small that they no longer play a significant impact on the acoustics.

“Everything above the Schroeder frequency mostly concerns reflection control and broadband absorption, which are much easier to predict and deal with. Wave lengths are shorter, and therefore behave much more like straight lines, like balls bouncing around on a pool table. But predicting acoustic behaviour below 200-300Hz remains the most difficult part of our work.”

“Below Schroeder, prediction becomes much more complex in small rooms. Over the years we’ve been using several tools, ranging from intuition, to spreadsheets, formulas, histograms, and software, but usually focused on SBIR (speaker boundary interference analysis) and axial mode relationships. Our research and observations over the past 5 years have confirmed that we can do better. We wanted to develop software that could predict acoustic behaviour at low end frequencies, and this led us to start a research project.”.

WDSG is not known for taking half measures, and so it formed an entire company to deal with the low-frequency challenge, as Storyk explains. “Our internal research team essentially morphed into an entire company called REDI Acoustics, ultimately creating its own proprietary software called NIRO. We love acronyms! REDI stands for Research Education Development Initiative, and NIRO for Non-cuboid, Iterative Room Optimization.”

“REDI started with two people, and then we hired a third one and then another one, and so on. We threw a little money at it, and then some more, and we now have something that is much better at predicting the acoustic behaviour of low frequencies in a critical listening environment. Rue Boyer was one of the first projects we used NIRO on, and since then we have used the software for all our projects, and even for non-WSDG projects. We continue to want proof of the concept and more accurate verification, so we want more people to adopt this process.”

1 GEOMETRY MODULE

Optimizes the room shape, loudspeaker, and listener positions to achieve an optimal acoustic response within the architectural constraints.

2 DAMPER MODULE

Uses APEQs™ (Acoustic Parametric EQualizers) to calculate the optimum acoustical performance of your room.

3 REFLECTION MODULE

Analyses all the sound pathways inside the room to assess how to control reflections.

“NIRO is not a magic box. It involves several steps. The first is geometric optimization. This is the first step in any room design. It does not only mean looking at walls, but also the shape of the room, the positioning of the sound sources and listening places, and so on. We’re particularly interested in the positioning of any subs, again stressing the all-important below-Schroeder-frequency analysis.

“In this initial step, we essentially tell the software what we have and what we can change geometrically. What if we make the room a metre wider, or the ceiling a metre higher? What if we move the listening position? What if we change the position of the sub? It takes some work to input all the data, because the program is in Python, and not particularly user-friendly. NIRO does not have a front end at this time, because we’re not interested in selling it. Our deliverables are detailed reports.”

“Once NIRO has all the data, we start running the program, using a generic algorithm to optimize the best combination of variables. It performs hundreds, sometimes thousands, of quick calculations, as it spits out frequency responses, analyzing room modes and SBIR optimisation. Sometimes it takes a few hours, sometimes it has to run overnight. It depends on the amount of variables. At some point we get the best geometrically optimized positioning of all the variables.”

“Then we get to part two, which is using BEM [Boundary Element Method] to slice the boundaries up in small pieces, and figure out where the pressure points are, and we start inserting targeted pressure absorbers in these positions. They’re intelligent guesses. Then we run the software again to see how these pressure absorbers influence the frequency responses. Sometimes we run the software 10 more times, sometimes 20 more times. The Holy Grail is that this process becomes automatic.”

The next step is to apply NIRO's calculations to the real world. Storyk elaborates, “There are only a few ways in which one can efficiently absorb low-frequency energy in a small room. Either you build resonators, which are essentially thin surfaces with holes or slots in them, typically made out of wood, or membrane absorbers, which are thinner diaphragmatic surfaces that move, or big, thick, fluffy volumes, like good old-fashioned traps.”

PERFORATED RESONATORS

MEMBRANE ABSORBERS

“Traps take space, and rooms are getting smaller, so we tend to not use them anymore. We prefer resonators now, because they are relatively cheap to build and very accurate to predict. Another Holy Grail in this context is a tuneable membrane absorber, also called an APEQ, or Acoustic Parametric EQ. We’ve created several prototypes, but we are not ready for prime time just yet.”

“You have to remember that all this is happening in the context of a studio being fitted with a desk, couches, racks, fish tanks, lights, doors, and all the objects and equipment that is in a room. Lots of things are changing all the time and there are many variables. This is where the art comes in.”

In many respects, Rue Boyer was an early testing ground for WSDG’s NIRO analysis initiative, though there were additional issues that complicated matters. “We inherited some fundamental infrastructure in the building in Paris that could not be changed,” recalls Storyk. “But of course, that’s not the first time for us. We also inherited a little bit of acoustic design, but we decided to restart from scratch.”

THE FINAL DESIGN

“The first thing we did was test the shape of the main room for low frequency response, and then we worked with that in NIRO. Using NIRO, and other pieces of software, and our experience and intuition, allowed us to make sure that the main room we were working in became a world-class control room. It also helped us to fine-tune where we could position key project components in the room, such as the large console, that retracts into the floor on a motorised lift—a first for our design team!”

“We ended up with eight to ten different treatments in Rue Boyer Studio A, and then our goal, as designers and artists, is to create an environment in such a way that you don’t realise these things are there. In this room, the resonators use wood as the primary material, due to Max and Victor’s requirement of a vintage look. By contrast, we also had some projects where the owner wanted all the treatments to be highly visible, almost looking like a lab. That’s the other extreme. Such is life in our fast lane!”

“The last step of the NIRO process is tuning. Designing studios has always been about architecture, acoustics, art, and audio. We acknowledge all Four A’s. Tuning, or audio, is the process where you put your signature on a room. Every room, whether we believe it or not, has a signature. The signature can be that of the owner, who wants the room adjusted to what their ears tell them. Who am I, or we, or any of us, to argue with Jack Antonoff or Tchad Blake or Paul Epworth or Eddie Kramer, or other friends or clients? However, if a studio doesn’t have an owner that is capable, one of our engineers becomes the author of that studio’s audio signature.”

EDDIE KRAMER WITH ACE FREHLEY OF KISS AND HIS BAND
NEW YORK 1987

“I remember many years ago turning a knob on a console when Eddie Kramer was working on a track by a big rock group. I think it was Kiss. Eddie couldn’t see what I was doing, and I said, ‘Eddie, can you hear the difference?’ He says, ‘Yeah, I can hear it,’ and he told me exactly what I’d just done: I had added a little level on the EQ strip to the mid-range of a guitar. I couldn’t hear any difference. It all sounded terrific to me. But these engineers are that good. Our job is to make a studio that allows them to do what they need to do.”
